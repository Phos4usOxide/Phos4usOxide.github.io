Audio

User interfaces and websites are mainly visual, but audio can improve the user experience. It can also improve accessibility by allowing users to retain information through sound in case they have trouble visually. Examples of audio include sound effects, music, and speech, all with their own usages and advantages. Let's check out some information and guidelines for audio usage!

Usage

Audio is mainly used for sound effects, music, and speech. Sound effects can be played upon specific actions, like a noise upon clicking a button, a camera shutter when taking a picture using an app, or how Outlook.com plays a "ding" sound when you receive an email. Music isn't that common, but it can be played in the background of a game or when waiting for something to load. Speech can be used to read text on the page or to have someone talk about something relevant to the text.

Quality

Ensure the audio has a good volume and pitch so it isn't too loud or annoying. The audio should be clear so the user knows exactly what the sound is or what the speech is saying. Lastly, make sure the audio has a good bit depth so it isn't muffled or robotic sounding.

Accessibility & Options

For speech, when applicable, subtitles or a transcript should be available in multiple languages to allow users to know what's being said, like with videos. Also when applicable, options should exist for the sound to be paused, played, replayed, and stopped. Options should also exist to change the volume, mute the sound, and change the playback speed for user preference.
